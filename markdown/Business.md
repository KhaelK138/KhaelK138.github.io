## Business Notes

**Number 1 reason companies go out of business is running out of money**
- Can be clever to secure a line of credit with a bank like JPMC for some amount of money per month, just in case of worst-case scenario

**Getting more work from existing work**
- Always make sure to take notes during the engagement to see where we'd want to go next with the customer
- Asking customer constantly "When do you expect to have this done by?" for the Purchase Order (PO)
- In big calls, can consider reintroducing the company and what we offer before going into the specific call information
  - This can help sell other service offerings, like selling unrelated AI on a red team sales call

**Start big, take it smaller, give customer optionality**
- If the customer doesn't want or can't afford a red team based on budget, then definitely try and do something smaller
- If a red team is $150k but all they have is $80k, we can do an assumed breach instead, which saves them money on the social engineering and such 
- If low on time, can always record a video discussing the options and how much they cost, which we can send to the client

## Starting a Business

**Everything, EVERYTHING, is minimizing risk**
- Whether that's risk for yourself, for prospective or existing clients, for shareholders, for employees, for advisors
- Examples:
  - Funders: Prospective funders will be much more likely to invest if you show clients who are committed to the product once finished
  - Advisors: Advisors will be much more inclined to help if positive progress with potential clients are shown
  - Employees: It is a lot easier to hire someone as a contractor to see how they work, and then as an employee
  - Self: Moonlight a business idea before jumping ship to it
    - Talk to prospective clients and get even a verbal "yes I'd be interested" or "yes I'll track progress" can decrease risk
  - Business: Make big decisions smaller to minimize risk, and make small decisions quickly
  - Customers: It is a lot less risky for a customer to try out a product for a month, whereas a more established business may only offer annual

**Getting Started - Criteria**
- Go with the best idea you have at the time
  - The best idea now might not be the best idea in a year (or someone may take the idea)
- Always make sure to define the criteria of the business you want to create before creating it
  - If cofounding, almost never cofound 50:50
- This could include the work-life balance, the poeple you'll hire, the culture you'd want to build
  - This defined culture should also be communicated to potential employees
    - One startup would even call the partners of potential employees at the END of the process to confirm that the culture fit their expectations
- Also define where the business sites on the "fuck-with" scale
  - (money) x (fuck-with) / (time) = worth making a business
  - For example, if a business doesn't make much money, but we heavily fuck with it, that's ok
  - If a business idea doens't really fit our fuck-with scale, we can give it to someone else and be an advisor
- Determine product-market fit AND product-"offer" fit
  - Does the product fit the market?

**Relying on Networks**
- Oftentimes, starting out early means a smaller network
- This is where advisors can be leaned on - looking for advisors with large networks in the field can really help network, especially with clients
  - Advisors can even become cofounders
- Sell to other founders, as it's easy to sympathize and they often move a lot faster than bigger companies

**Funding**
- Important questions to answer before getting outside funding:
  - Do we have three paths besides raising capital to fund the company?
  - Can we deal with the overhead of raising capital?
  - Is self-funding possible?
  - What rate of growth could be supported without outside capital?
- Main funding categories are via sales, non-dilutive, debt, equity, and revenue share
- Sales - always the best way to fund a company
  - Gross-Margin Profit via sales - try asking potential customers for prepayments/early deposits or upcharge premium service offerings
  - Factoring: Third party buys unpaid invoices or purchase orders at a slight discount, which gives cash on-hand
  - License specific non-core pieces of IP/technology to foreign companies or to markets/industries we aren't targeting
    - Could also license internal processes to a competitor in a different geographic region for access to customer list
  - Donation crowdfunding (like Kickstarter): Give people the option to pre-buy a product 
  - Consulting: leverage core expertise to consult until the product is generating cash (not just GTM ready)
- Non-dilutive - using our and others' existing client-bases
  - Affiliate deal: Market another company's product to existing customer base for split cash or access to their audience
  - Cost-per-action: Hire firms that only receive payment upon a successful action (a sale/download/signup)
  - Deal Lead Exchange: Multiple companies exchange leads with no cost to any
  - Bartering: offer anything we can for anything we need, like office space or web traffic - this could be equity, but it could also be services
  - Corporate Investment: 
    - Non-Recurring Engineering (NRE): corporation pays to build/customize something for them, funding R&D and ideally allowing retainment of IP
    - Market Development Funds (MDF): Some companies may have budgets for helping partners enter new markets, so we can ask to partner and do the venture work for them
    - Co-marketing: Other companies may be willing to pay us to acquire the customer for both of us
    - License/Lend Technology: Some services are free or heavily subsidized for small business, in the hope that they land a customer
      - AWS Activate offers startups up to $200K in credits to basically become the guaranteed infra of the customer if they get up and running
- Debt - make SURE to only take on debt with a cash flow
  - Credit lines
    - Can ask to tie payments to our collections/revenue cycle
  - Friends/family
  - Bank lines: very rare to get without a personal guarantee (PG, meaning if the business fails we're on the hook) - try to avoid PG, or if mandatory, carve out things like house
- Equity - giving ownership in exchange for capital
  - Angel investors: Invest their own money in exchange for equity
    - Best fit is someone who knows the industry, us personally, or has a track record of early-stage investing
    - There are networks of angel investors, which can be up to a few million if the deal is right
  - Pre-seed networks: profesionally-managed mini-VCs focusing on very early-stage, though they do often require demonstrated users/revenue/proof-of-concept
  - Equity crowdfunding (like StartEngine): sell small equity stakes to large number of regular investors online

**Getting a big deal**
- The way you land a big deal is by having previous soft commits on the idea
- The most common method of getting a business onto its feet would be finding one big client to supply steady revenue as we find clients
- If you sell a SAAS product, this could mean leveraging your network to sell a multi-year contract
  - Alternatively, this can be supplied by venture capitalism, which also provides a network for companies
- Prospective customers should be shown the insatiable hunger to win
  - "This is literally the only thing that I do, I will absolutely make sure that you get value"

## Being a PM

**Being the Glue**
- If someone isn't the stopgap, people will have to do things outside of their roles
- Thus, PMs will often have to take this support role, changing when necessary

**Time Commitment**
- Almost all time spent will be in meetings, putting out fires, and in 1:1s
- Often, the correct answer is delegation

**Assigned to Clients**
- It does make sense for PMs to own clients, as they can keep engagements consistent for clients
- PMs also manage sales for most companies

**Sales**
- Let customers know what other types of engagements we do at the end of an engagement

**Hard Conversations**
- Be radically transparent
  - It can be difficult to tell someone something, but it can help to keep in mind how much else in the business suffers if the hard conversation isn't had. The other engineers, the customers, the PMs, etc.
- Then follow it up and ask how to help
- Break progress down into steps
